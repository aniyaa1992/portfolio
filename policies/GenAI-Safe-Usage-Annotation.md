Portfolio Annotation

 
What is this document?


This Generative AI Safe Usage Policy was written as a deliverable during my previous role as a security risk advisor with the goal of developing a reusable professional framework. It was actively deployed across my individual book of business, which included 45 clients spanning different industries, and served as a structured starting point that each client then adapted to their specific regulatory environment, technology stack, and operational risk profile. This policy was also adopted as the standardized template organization-wide at my previous firm. The [Client Organization] placeholder reflects this reusable design: I intentionally wrote the policy to be industry-agnostic while remaining specific enough to be immediately useful without the need for executives to extensively rewrite.
The document itself has been sanitized for portfolio use. All employer and client identifying information has been removed. All policy content is unmodified from the professional deliverable.


What problem does this document solve?


From 2024-2025, over 90% of the organizations in my book of business were deploying generative AI tools faster than their governance and legal teams could evaluate the risks. This policy was designed to fill that gap, which affected nearly every industry simultaneously: the absence of enforceable, operationally practical AI usage rules that employees could realistically understand and follow. The framework addresses the four primary risk domains of AI governance: data privacy and security, intellectual property and copyright, bias and fairness, and transparency and disclosure. At the same time, it still remains lightweight enough for organizations to adopt and customize without lengthy internal approval.

What decisions did I make and why?

My Open/Closed AI System distinction in the definitions section was a deliberate structural choice: most employees and end-users at the time had no definitive reference point for why pasting company data into ChatGPT was categorically different from using a provisioned enterprise tool like Copilot, and that distinction needed to be named plainly before the policy rules would make sense. The High-Risk AI System Use section covering hiring, promotion, compensation, and retention decisions was included proactively based on EEOC guidance and emerging state-level AI legislation, not because any of my clients had experienced an incident in that area. Embedding it early meant clients who deployed the policy in regulated industries (healthcare, financial services, higher education) had those protections already in place, to be further adapted as the legislative landscape regarding AI rapidly changes. The Prohibited Use section was written to be legally defensible, not just advisory. This framing mirrors standard employee Acceptable Use technology policy language specifically so that violations could be actioned under existing HR and legal procedures.


What does this demonstrate?


Writing policies that work across industries requires a different skill than writing policies for a single organization. A policy that is too specific becomes irrelevant the moment the client's technology stack or regulatory context differs. A policy that’s too generic provides no real guidance. This policy demonstrates my ability to balance those requirements: writing rules that are specific enough to be enforceable and operationally actionable enterprise-wide, while remaining adaptable enough that a healthcare organization, a financial services firm, and a manufacturing company could all adopt the same framework and tailor it without needing to rewrite it from scratch. It also demonstrates my awareness of the AI governance landscape at a time when organizations were then (and continue to now) scrambling to roll out AI and develop governance and policy in this space.


Common industry-specific adaptations to be included: expanding the Data Privacy and Security section with PHI handling requirements and HIPAA minimum necessary standards for healthcare clients and hospital networks; extending the Prohibited Use list for financial services clients subject to SEC or FINRA recordkeeping obligations that require AI-generated communications and research outputs to carry additional retention and supervisory review requirements; adjusting the Training and Awareness section based on whether the client had an existing security awareness program or needed a framework defined from scratch; and adding procurement and vendor review language for clients in regulated industries requiring third-party AI tools pass formal vendor risk assessment before approval for internal use.
